XLOG's neural-symbolic layer lets a program mix two kinds of knowledge: rules you
write and rules the engine learns. A **neural predicate** turns a PyTorch network into
a logical relation whose facts carry probabilities; **differentiable ILP** searches a
space of candidate Datalog clauses with gradient descent and promotes the winner into
your program. Both share the compiled circuit and the same autograd bridge, so a loss
computed on a symbolic query flows all the way back into network weights.

<Note>
The entire neural-symbolic surface — neural predicates, differentiable ILP, and
exact induction — ships **only** through the `pyxlog` PyPI wheel, which tracks the
`v0.9.2` release. It is a Python-first API; the published crates on crates.io do not
expose it. Everything on this page marked "released" means "present in the latest
`pyxlog` wheel."
</Note>

## Neural predicates

A neural predicate declares that a relation's truth is decided by a network rather than
by stated facts. You write the declaration in the program with `::`, binding a
registered network name to a predicate head:

```xlog
nn(coin_net, [X], Y, [heads, tails]) :: coin(X, Y).
```

This is the four-argument form, `nn/4`. It reads: for input `X`, the network
`coin_net` produces a distribution over the labels `[heads, tails]`, and that
distribution becomes the probability of `coin(X, heads)` and `coin(X, tails)`. The
presence of a label list makes it a **classification** predicate — the network output
is a categorical label distribution.

Drop the label list and you get the three-argument form, `nn/3`, an **embedding**
predicate:

```xlog
nn(embed_net, [X], E) :: node_embedding(X, E).
```

Here the network maps an input to a learned vector `E` rather than to a label
distribution. A given network may be declared in one form or the other, but not both —
declaring the same name as both classification and embedding is rejected at compile
time.

<Note>
Declarations are validated when the program compiles: input and output variables must
be disjoint, every argument must appear in the bound predicate, and anonymous or
aggregate variables are not allowed in a neural declaration.
</Note>

## Registering networks

The declaration names a network; you supply the actual `torch.nn.Module` from Python.
Registration is where the PyTorch autograd bridge is wired up.

- `register_network(name, module, optimizer, scheduler=None, ...)` binds a classifier
  or `nn/4` head. You pass the module and its optimizer (and optionally an LR
  scheduler); the bridge holds them and drives forward and backward passes through the
  circuit. Batching, top-`k` truncation, determinism, and a call cache are configurable
  here.
- `register_embedding(name, module_or_tensor, trainable=True)` binds an `nn/3`
  embedding — either a module or a plain tensor of vectors. Set `trainable=False` to
  freeze it.

Once registered, evaluating a query that touches the predicate runs the network,
converts its output into circuit leaf probabilities, and — during training — pushes
gradients back through those leaves into the module's parameters. The bridge exposes
`forward_backward(query, expected=True)`, several differentiable losses
(`belnap_loss`, `semantic_loss_tensor`, `mse_loss_tensor`), and epoch drivers
(`train_epoch`, `train_model`) with optional validation-set early stopping.

## Differentiable ILP

Writing a neural predicate assumes you already know the rule. Differentiable ILP is for
the opposite case: you have examples of what should be true and want the engine to
**discover the clause**. XLOG frames this as learning which body to attach to a head.

You mark a clause as learnable with a mask annotation:

```xlog
learnable(mask) :: reach(X, Y) :- edge(X, Z), edge(Z, Y).
```

Rather than compile one candidate rule at a time — impossible at training timescales —
XLOG pre-compiles a **tensorized super-graph** containing every syntactically legal
candidate body at once, then activates them with a continuous mask tensor. Each
candidate gets a logit; a **Gumbel-Softmax** relaxation with temperature `τ` turns the
logits into a soft, differentiable selection over candidates. The circuit evaluates
under that soft mask, a loss compares the derived facts against the examples, and the
gradient updates the logits.

As training proceeds, the temperature anneals toward a floor on a cosine schedule,
sharpening the soft mask toward a one-hot choice. At convergence the engine takes
`argmax` over the logits: the winning candidate is decoded back into a concrete Datalog
clause — a discrete rule string, not a soft mixture.

### Sparse and dense mask backends

How the mask reaches the executor is chosen by a backend:

| Backend | Parameters | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| `SparseMaskBackend` (default) | `O(C)` — one logit per candidate | Production. Ranks candidates on device and pushes only the selected sparse set; no cubic tensor is materialized. |
| `DenseMaskBackend` | `O(N³)` — full schema cube | Debugging and parity checks. Enable with `debug_dense_mask=True`; materializes the whole mask, so it is expensive on large schemas. |

The sparse backend is the reason training stays inside the GPU-resident hot loop:
candidate soft-probabilities are ranked and applied without downloading a full mask
vector to the host.

## The Python training API

Two entry points drive learning, both in `pyxlog.ilp`.

`train_only(...)` runs the search: it enumerates the legal candidates, launches several
independent restarts from fresh logits, and for each restart iterates the step loop —
apply the mask, evaluate on the device, compute the loss, back-propagate, step the
optimizer, anneal `τ`, and stop early once the `argmax` is stable and the loss is below
threshold. It returns the discovered rule and its training telemetry.

`train_and_promote(...)` wraps `train_only` with a set of **promotion gates**. A
discovered rule is only committed into your program if it clears every gate:

| Gate | What it checks |
|---|---|
| Convergence | Training actually reached a stable `argmax`. |
| Novel-rate | The rule does not over-derive facts outside the examples. |
| Protected-relation | It produces no unwanted side effects on protected relations. |
| Holdout F1 | F1 on held-out examples meets the threshold (leave-one-out for small sets, k-fold for larger). |
| Ambiguity | No alternative candidate is a near-equal winner. |
| Typed-schema | Relation type metadata is present (or a reviewed waiver is supplied). |

Only a rule that passes all gates is written into the committed source; anything short
of that is reported with the gate it failed, never silently promoted.

## Reliability gates

Because the search is stochastic, dILP is held to seed-level reliability gates that
grow stricter as the subsystem matures:

| Maturity | Gate | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Alpha | `5/5` | Five consecutive `train_only` runs must all converge. |
| Beta | `20/20` | 5 seeds across each of 4 stages (reach, grandparent, colleague, plus-2) on the sparse backend — all 20 converge. |
| GA | `50/50` | 50 seeds pass with a Clopper–Pearson lower bound on the success rate. |

<Warning>
The GA gate is `50/50` evaluated with a **Clopper–Pearson** lower confidence bound —
not a loose "50-seed run," and there is no `200/200` gate anywhere in the suite.
Quote these numbers exactly.
</Warning>

## A runnable `nn/4` example

This is a self-contained DeepProbLog-style program: a small classifier decides whether
each coin image shows heads or tails, symbolic rules define winning and losing, and the
network is trained purely from the derived symbolic queries. The synthetic tensors let
it run without a dataset download.

```xlog
nn(coin_net, [X], Y, [heads, tails]) :: coin(X, Y).

win(X, Y)  :- coin(X, heads), coin(Y, heads).
lose(X, Y) :- coin(X, tails).
lose(X, Y) :- coin(Y, tails).
```

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import pyxlog

DEVICE = "cuda" if torch.cuda.is_available() else "cpu"

# 2-class classifier over 3x64x64 coin images -> {heads, tails}
class CoinNet(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Flatten(), nn.Linear(3 * 64 * 64, 64), nn.ReLU(), nn.Linear(64, 2)
        )
    def forward(self, x):
        return torch.log_softmax(self.net(x), dim=-1)  # log-probs over [heads, tails]

# Compile the program and bind the network to the nn(coin_net, ...) declaration.
program = pyxlog.Program.compile(open("coins.xlog").read())
net = CoinNet().to(DEVICE)
opt = torch.optim.Adam(net.parameters(), lr=1e-3)
program.register_network("coin_net", net, opt)

# Synthetic labelled coin images (even idx -> heads, odd idx -> tails).
labels = [i % 2 for i in range(16)]                    # 0=heads, 1=tails
images = torch.zeros(16, 3, 64, 64, device=DEVICE)
for i, y in enumerate(labels):
    images[i, y] = 1.0                                 # class-separable signal
program.add_tensor_source("train", images)

# Supervised atoms: coin(i, heads|tails). The label distribution is trained
# by back-propagating NLL through the circuit.
classes = ["heads", "tails"]
queries = [f"coin({i}, {classes[y]})" for i, y in enumerate(labels)]

for epoch in range(10):
    program.train_epoch(queries, batch_size=8)

# Inference: query a derived symbolic head backed by the trained network.
print(program.query("win(0, 2)"))   # both even -> both heads -> fires high
print(program.query("lose(1, 3)"))  # odd -> tails -> lose fires high
```

The optimizer here is Adam; on the multiplicative loss surface plain SGD tends to
plateau, so Adam is the practical default.

## On the development branch (unreleased)

The following extend the neural-symbolic surface but are **not** in the latest
`pyxlog` wheel. They exist on the development branch only and should not be relied on in
released code.

<Warning>
Available on the development branch; not in the `v0.9.2` release. Treat this section as
a preview of in-progress work.
</Warning>

- **Existential-join trainable bodies (Stage B).** A trainable body may join a neural
  predicate to an ordinary relation on a non-head variable; the neural predicate is
  grounded over the real join domain inside the circuit and OR-aggregated at the head.
  Per-event features arrive through a `domain_inputs=` channel and
  `register_domain_tensor_source`. The join domain must be ground facts, head-binding
  ids must be `0..N-1`, and only a single join network is supported, with the planted
  graph bounded (roughly six to seven events) by a fixed circuit buffer.
- **Joint multi-rule mixtures.** Several same-head trainable clauses can be learned
  jointly as a guard-only noisy-OR mixture, with `evaluate_joint_mixture` reading the
  held-out result. Neural-carrying candidates beyond the guard remain a documented
  follow-up.
- **GPU-resident zero-host training step.** `forward_backward_grouped(queries,
  expected)` performs one host synchronization per step instead of a per-query
  round-trip, keeping the training loop resident on the device.

<Note>
The higher-level neuro-symbolic driver
(`pyxlog.ilp.neurosymbolic.train_neurosymbolic_program`, with declarative
`trainable_rule(...)` / `train(...)` in-source) is where the Stage-B `domain_inputs=`
channel is exposed. It, too, is a development-branch surface.
</Note>
